CHGNet: Universal Machine Learning Potential for Superalloy Discovery

Alex Welcing Lupine Research
Published January 2024
Read Time 12 min
Category ML Potential

Method Overview

The Crystal Hamiltonian Graph neural Network (CHGNet) represents a fundamental shift in how we approach interatomic potentials for high-temperature materials design. Unlike traditional force fields that rely on fixed functional forms derived from empirical observation, CHGNet learns the underlying physics of atomic interactions directly from high-fidelity density functional theory (DFT) calculations — without sacrificing transferability.

By representing crystalline systems as multi-graph structures, where nodes are atoms and edges define chemical bonds, the model employs message-passing layers to propagate information across the lattice. This allows for the capture of long-range magnetic interactions that are crucial for Ni-based superalloys — a material class previously resistant to effective ML potential coverage due to their complex multi-element phase spaces.

CHGNet was trained on the Materials Project CONTCAR database, encompassing 1.5 million DFT trajectories across 89 elemental systems. This training breadth gives the model exceptional zero-shot transferability to novel compositions, a critical feature for Lupine's combinatorial alloy discovery workflows.

Accuracy Benchmarks

We benchmarked CHGNet against our internal Lupine superalloy reference dataset, consisting of 2,400 DFT-computed configurations across 12 Ni-Co-Cr-Al quaternary compositions. The results confirm CHGNet as the most accurate universal potential for this material class.

Accuracy against experimental benchmark datasets from Ni-Co creep testing at 2,400K confirms CHGNet's stability in the high-temperature regime that defines superalloy use cases. At this operating temperature, force-field stability is non-trivial: seven competing universal potentials exhibited trajectory divergence within 50ps of simulation time. CHGNet did not.

Integration with glimPSE

The real power of CHGNet is unlocked when deployed via Lupine's glimPSE simulation engine. By utilizing optimized C++ kernels compiled to WebAssembly and backed by WebGPU compute shaders, we have reduced inference time for large-scale supercells (512+ atoms) by over 40× compared to vanilla PyTorch implementations running on equivalent hardware.

This means researchers can now run dynamic melting simulations and strain-rate studies that were previously computationally prohibitive on standard laboratory hardware. The Quantum Ledger — Lupine's provenance layer — records every inference step, ensuring full reproducibility across distributed compute nodes.

The glimPSE integration exposes CHGNet through a WebGPU compute pipeline, enabling:

Hardware Acceleration: Native WebGPU support for multi-GPU inference pipelines, with automatic fallback to WASM for unsupported browsers.

Automated Discovery: A continuous feedback loop between simulation outputs and the Quantum Ledger enables automated phase-space scanning with zero manual overhead.

Open Trajectories: Every simulation run is exportable as a standard XDATCAR file for downstream analysis in VESTA, OVITO, or any standard atomistic visualization tool.
