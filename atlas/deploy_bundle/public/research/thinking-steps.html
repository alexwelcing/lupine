While systematic benchmarking databases have quantified the performance of these models, the geometric and statistical structure of their prediction errors remains poorly understood. Here we show that classical force field errors are not unstructured noise, but rather occupy strictly bounded, low-dimensional manifolds, and that standard cross-element benchmarking inadvertently suffers from Simpson’s paradox. By synthesizing sloppy model theory with causal inference and meta-analytic methodology, we demonstrate that prediction errors for face-centered cubic (FCC) elastic constants ($C_{11}, C_{12}, C_{44}$) are compressed onto a hyper-ribbon with an effective dimensionality of ~1.66 out of 3. Furthermore, we reveal that indiscriminately pooling body-centered cubic (BCC) benchmark data across different elements produces a spurious correlation inversion—an ecological fallacy driven by element identity acting as a causal confounder. Correcting this requires random-effects meta-analysis and Fisher z-transformations. Our results explain the systematic structural constraints inherent in empirical potentials and provide a rigorous mathematical framework for diagnosing why modern universal neural network potentials exhibit systematic physical biases. These findings fundamentally revise how materials science AI models must be trained and evaluated, shifting the paradigm from bulk error minimization to causal, manifold-aware model reduction.The deployment of interatomic potentials—ranging from computationally efficient Embedded-Atom Method (EAM) models to highly parameterized, universal deep neural networks—is bottlenecked by the difficulty of quantifying parametric uncertainty. High-throughput benchmarking platforms such as OpenKIM and JARVIS-FF have standardized the computation of critical properties, yet the resulting prediction errors are typically treated as isotropic, uncorrelated noise. However, recent empirical observations reveal that errors in macroscopic predictions, such as elastic constants, are heavily correlated across disparate potential families. Furthermore, state-of-the-art universal MLIPs trained on vast datasets exhibit systematic physical distortions, such as anomalous potential energy surface (PES) softening. We propose that these persistent artifacts are structural inevitabilities dictated by the convergence of sloppy model theory and causal confounding.The low-dimensional error manifoldFirst developed in computational biology and statistical physics, sloppy model theory establishes that multiparameter models generically have parameter sensitivities spanning many orders of magnitude. Mapping this parameter space to the space of physical predictions yields a bounded manifold—a "hyper-ribbon" characterized by a geometric hierarchy of widths. We argue that interatomic potentials belong to this same universality class. Jacobian-based error propagation transfers this low-dimensional covariance directly into derived engineering moduli (e.g., bulk and Voigt shear moduli), ensuring that macroscopic simulation errors are inherently deterministic.Causal confounding and Simpson’s paradoxIf errors are structurally bounded by functional forms, their statistical evaluation across large materials databases introduces a severe vulnerability. We demonstrate that this approach fundamentally violates causal inference principles, manifesting as Simpson’s paradox.When analyzing predicted elastic constant errors across body-centered cubic (BCC) metals (e.g., Mo, W, Fe, V), we observe a direct correlation inversion. The error vector for a given potential evaluating a material is defined as $\Delta \mathbf{C} = [\Delta C_{11}, \Delta C_{12}, \Delta C_{44}]^T$. We compute the empirical error covariance matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$. To extract the effective dimensionality without arbitrary variance thresholds, we apply the participation ratio (PR), derived from condensed matter physics for Anderson mode localization:$$ \text{PR} = \frac{\left(\sum_{k=1}^3 \lambda_k\right)^2}{\sum_{k=1}^3 \lambda_k^2} $$where $\lambda_k$ are the eigenvalues of $\Sigma$. A PR of 3 indicates isotropic, independent errors, whereas $\text{PR} \to 1$ indicates total restriction to a 1D line. The transformation of these intrinsic errors to derived engineering moduli (Bulk modulus $K$, Voigt Shear modulus $G_V$) is calculated via standard Jacobian error propagation ($\Sigma_{\text{derived}} = \mathbf{J} \Sigma \mathbf{J}^T$), explicitly demonstrating how the null space of $\mathbf{J}$ aligns with the sloppy directions of the interatomic manifold.Causal deconfounding via Pearl's back-door adjustmentTo resolve Simpson's paradox in cross-element evaluations, we model the benchmarking process as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) where $\text{Element} \rightarrow \text{True Physics}$ and $\text{Element} \rightarrow \text{Potential Error Structure}$. Because Element is a common cause, evaluating $\Delta C_i$ vs. $\Delta C_j$ across the aggregate dataset opens a backdoor path, biasing the correlation. Following Pearl’s back-door criterion, we block this path by stratifying the data by the confounding variable (Element).Permutation testing for mathematical couplingBecause physical properties mathematically share common denominators and structural stability constraints, the standard statistical null hypothesis $\rho = 0$ is invalid. To test significance within elemental strata, we establish a proper non-zero baseline correlation using Jackson and Somers' (1991) randomization protocols. We execute $10^4$ permutations of the $\Delta C_{ij}$ components to generate a null distribution of derived correlations inherent to cubic stability constraints.Random-effects meta-analysis for correlation integrationTo recover the universal true error correlation across disparate datasets, we synthesize the intra-element correlations using a random-effects meta-analytic framework (Hedges and Olkin, 1985; Borenstein et al., 2009). For each element $m$, we compute the within-group correlation $r_m$ and apply the Fisher $z$-transformation to stabilize the variance:$$ z_m = \text{arctanh}(r_m) $$The variance is given by $\text{Var}(z_m) = 1/(n_m - 3)$, where $n_m$ is the sample size for element $m$. The overall deconfounded correlation $Z_{\text{true}}$ is computed via inverse-variance weighting:$$ Z_{\text{true}} = \frac{\sum_{m} W_m z_m}{\sum_{m} W_m}, \quad \text{where } W_m = \frac{1}{\text{Var}(z_m) + \tau^2} $$Here, $\tau^2$ represents the between-element heterogeneity estimated via the DerSimonian-Laird method. The corrected causal correlation is obtained by back-transformation: $r_{\text{true}} = \tanh(Z_{\text{true}})$.
